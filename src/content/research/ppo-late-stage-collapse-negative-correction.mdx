## Summary

My initial question was simple:

**Why does PPO sometimes collapse abruptly in late-stage locomotion learning, even after a stable gait has already been formed?**

The standard interpretation of PPO is intuitive:
positive advantage increases the probability of an action, and negative advantage decreases it.
At the surface level, this suggests gradual improvement or at least gradual degradation.

However, actual locomotion training often behaves differently.
A policy can form a strong gait and then suddenly collapse into meaningless motion or complete failure, sometimes without recovering afterward.

## Core Hypothesis

My initial hypothesis was that, in locomotion, **negative advantage is not necessarily reliable directional evidence**.

For a Gaussian policy,

$$
\pi_\theta(a \mid s)=\mathcal{N}(\mu_\theta(s), \sigma_\theta(s)^2)
$$

and the mean-direction interpretation of the policy gradient can be written as

$$
g_\mu \propto \hat A_t \frac{(a_t-\mu)}{\sigma^2}.
$$

This means:

- if $\hat A_t > 0$, the mean is pulled toward the sampled action
- if $\hat A_t < 0$, the mean is pushed away from the sampled action

The problem is that in locomotion, value is not necessarily smooth with respect to action.
Because of contact timing, balance thresholds, friction, and coordination effects, a bad action does **not** always imply that moving the mean in the opposite direction is better.

So my initial concern was:

$$
\text{negative sample} \neq \text{reliable directional evidence}.
$$

## Proposed Fix

Based on that idea, I tested a modified PPO update that weakened or blocked mean correction in selected negative-advantage ranges.

The basic intention was:

- weak negative samples: do not directly repel the mean
- medium negative samples: reduce directional correction and emphasize exploration
- strong negative samples: keep stronger correction

The expectation was that this would reduce late-stage instability by preventing unnecessary destructive repulsion.

## Result

The result did not support the intervention.

Training did not improve meaningfully, and in many cases the policy became easier to break.
Instead of improving stability, the modified update often reduced recovery strength.

## What I Learned

The most important lesson was that my original concern was **partly correct but incomplete**.

The following statement still seems meaningful:

$$
\text{value space is not fully smooth} \Rightarrow
\text{negative advantage is imperfect directional evidence}.
$$

But I underestimated another important fact:

**PPO does not only improve by reinforcing good actions.  
It also recovers from slightly degraded states through corrective updates.**

That means negative correction is not only a possible source of instability.
It is also part of the policy's recovery mechanism.

So if negative correction is removed too aggressively, the update loses not only harmful repulsion, but also part of its ability to return from local degradation.

## Revised Interpretation

My revised conclusion is:

1. Negative samples are not perfect directional evidence.
2. But removing negative correction too strongly also removes part of PPO's recovery ability.

So the better direction is not to eliminate negative mean correction, but to weaken it more carefully:

$$
g_\mu^{\mathrm{mod}} = \alpha(\tilde A)\, g_\mu^{\mathrm{ppo}}, \qquad 0 < \alpha(\tilde A) < 1
$$

rather than

$$
g_\mu^{\mathrm{mod}} = 0
$$

over a broad negative range.

## Final Conclusion

This experiment did not validate my proposed solution.
But it clarified something important:

**My initial hypothesis was useful as a problem statement, but too aggressive as a solution.**

The next step is to design a more balanced intervention that reduces destructive negative correction without destroying PPO's recovery pathway.
