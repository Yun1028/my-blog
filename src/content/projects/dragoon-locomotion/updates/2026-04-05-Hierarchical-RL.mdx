## Overview

After observing the limitations of a single flat policy on more decision-oriented maps, I designed a hierarchical reinforcement learning structure for the Dragoon robot.

The main idea was to separate two very different problems:

- choosing where to go
- learning how to move there

Instead of forcing one policy to simultaneously solve terrain interpretation, directional choice, and joint-level locomotion control, I split the system into a high-level policy and a low-level policy.

In this design, the high-level policy produces a 2D command vector, and the low-level policy learns to execute locomotion that follows that command.

## Motivation

The earlier stages mainly tested whether the robot could move forward and remain stable on structured terrain.
However, once the environment began to require turning, avoiding walls, and selecting alternative directions, I considered the original single-policy structure too inefficient.

My interpretation at the time was that the search space had become too large for a flat locomotion policy to handle efficiently.
This led me to a hierarchical formulation in which decision-making and motion execution were treated as different learning problems.

---

## Core Idea

The HRL structure is built around a shared latent representation and a 2D intermediate command.

### High-level policy

The high-level policy observes the environment and produces a 2D latent command `z`.

This command acts as a directional target in the plane.
It does not directly control the joints.
Instead, it represents a high-level movement intention such as which direction to move and how strongly to move in that direction.

### Low-level policy

The low-level policy receives both the shared encoded observation and the command `z`.
Its role is not to decide the route, but to generate stable joint actions that follow the given command.

This separates directional decision-making from actual locomotion execution.

---

## Observation Structure

The policy uses two observation branches:

- **Proprioceptive observation**
  - heading
  - base linear velocity
  - base angular velocity
  - projected gravity
  - joint position offsets
  - joint velocities
  - previous actions
  - foot contact states
  - previous high-level command

- **Terrain observation**
  - a local 101×101 height map around the robot

This means the system combines internal robot state and local terrain geometry in a multimodal observation design.

---

## Architecture

## Shared encoder

The high-level and low-level policies do not use fully separate encoders.
Instead, they share a common encoder composed of:

- modality-specific observation normalization
- a proprioceptive encoder
- a terrain encoder
- a fusion MLP

The proprioceptive branch compresses the robot state into a compact latent representation.
The terrain branch uses a convolutional encoder to extract features from the local height map.
These features are then concatenated and projected into a shared latent space.

This shared representation is used by both the high-level and low-level policies.

## High-level branch

The high-level branch is trained with SAC.

It takes the shared feature and outputs a Gaussian distribution over the 2D command `z`.
After sampling, the output is squashed and scaled to produce a bounded continuous high-level motion command.

The high-level critic is implemented with twin Q-networks.

## Low-level branch

The low-level branch is trained with PPO.

It takes the shared feature together with `z` and outputs continuous joint actions for the robot.
This branch is responsible for command-conditioned locomotion rather than route planning.

---

## Why SAC for the high level and PPO for the low level

I used different RL algorithms for the two levels because their roles were fundamentally different.

The high-level policy needed to make sparse, longer-horizon directional decisions in a continuous latent command space.
For that reason, I used SAC as the high-level learner.

The low-level policy, in contrast, needed to generate stable locomotion behavior at a finer control frequency.
For that role, PPO was more suitable as a command-following locomotion controller.

So this HRL design is not just a hierarchy of policies, but also a hierarchy of learning objectives and optimization styles.

---

## Command Interface: z

The central interface of this design is the 2D command `z`.

The high-level policy proposes `z`, and the environment manages the actual command used by the low-level controller.
During training, random command overrides were also inserted for fixed durations in order to teach the low-level policy to reliably follow externally given commands.

This was important because it allowed the low-level controller to first learn robust command tracking, instead of depending entirely on an already-correct high-level policy from the beginning.

---

## Reward Decomposition

The two levels are trained with different reward structures.

### Low-level reward

The low-level policy is rewarded for how well it follows the command:

- projected speed along `z`
- alignment with the commanded direction
- acceleration consistency
- upright stability
- low slip
- low action cost
- reduced stopping behavior

This makes the low-level objective primarily a command-tracking locomotion objective.

### High-level reward

The high-level reward is more task-oriented:

- forward progress
- success at reaching the goal
- penalties for falling
- penalties for failure-related episode termination

This means the high-level policy is optimized for choosing useful movement intentions rather than producing precise joint behavior.

---

## Temporal Hierarchy

The hierarchy also exists along the time axis.

The low-level policy acts at the normal control frequency.
The high-level policy updates the command more slowly, holding one command over a fixed interval before choosing the next one.

This turns the architecture into a true hierarchical controller with temporal abstraction, not just two policies stacked together.

---

## Why This Stage Matters

This stage was important because it was the first time I explicitly separated:

- directional decision making
- command-conditioned locomotion

Rather than treating locomotion and navigation as one inseparable policy problem, I introduced a structured interface between them.

This HRL design became a key step in thinking about how the robot should react when simple forward motion was no longer sufficient.
