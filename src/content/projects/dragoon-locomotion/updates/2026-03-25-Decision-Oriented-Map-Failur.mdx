## Overview

After the previous forward-only map experiment, I built a more difficult environment where simple straight locomotion was no longer sufficient.

This map was designed so that the robot had to do more than just move forward.  
It had to deal with walls, narrow ground, and cliff-like sections, which meant that the policy now needed some degree of situation-dependent decision making.

The main goal of this stage was to test whether the policy could go beyond basic locomotion and begin to choose appropriate actions depending on the local terrain structure.

## Environment Change

Compared to the earlier map, this environment introduced conditions where the robot could not solve the task by forward motion alone.

The map required the robot to:

- turn when it encountered a wall
- avoid falling from narrow platforms
- continue moving through more constrained terrain

This was the first stage where I tried to move from simple locomotion toward terrain-aware action selection.

---

## What I Thought at the Time

At the time, I interpreted the failure as an exploration and search-space problem.

The robot could still learn some degree of forward locomotion, but it did not learn to make the kind of directional decisions that the map required.
In particular, it failed to consistently choose behaviors such as turning away from a wall or finding an alternative path.

Because of this, I believed that a single flat RL policy was being asked to solve too many things at once:

- locomotion control
- terrain interpretation
- directional decision making
- path adjustment under constraints

That led me to think that the overall search space had become too large and that standard RL was inefficient for this kind of behavior.

This interpretation later became the motivation for exploring a hierarchical design, where high-level direction selection and low-level locomotion control could be separated.

---

## Policy Architecture Used in This Stage

The policy used a multimodal actor-critic architecture with separate encoders for proprioceptive state and terrain observations.

### Observation Structure

The input had two major branches:

- **Proprioceptive observation**
  - heading
  - base linear velocity
  - base angular velocity
  - projected gravity
  - joint position offsets
  - joint velocities
  - previous actions
  - foot contact states

- **Terrain observation**
  - a local height map around the robot

The proprioceptive branch represented the internal dynamic state of the robot, while the terrain branch represented the local geometric structure of the environment.

### Terrain Representation

The terrain input was encoded as a high-resolution local height map.
Instead of directly feeding raw terrain geometry into the control head, the map was first compressed through a convolutional encoder.

This branch was designed to preserve nearby terrain details while still reducing the spatial input into a compact latent representation suitable for policy learning.

### Proprioceptive Branch

The proprioceptive input was processed by a separate MLP-based encoder.
This branch focused on the robot's internal motion state and contact-related information.

Its role was different from the terrain branch:
rather than understanding the surrounding geometry, it modeled the robot's current body configuration and motion condition.

### Late Fusion Design

After separate encoding, the terrain feature and proprioceptive feature were concatenated and passed into fusion layers.

The actor and critic did not simply share a single final latent.
Instead, after the encoder stage, they used separate fusion paths and separate heads.
This made the architecture more suitable for learning policy and value representations without forcing both objectives to depend on the exact same fused features.

### Action Space

The policy was designed for continuous control of the dragoon robot.
It predicted continuous actions for all controllable joints through a stochastic actor, and the value function was learned through a separate critic stream.

---

## Why This Stage Mattered

This stage was important because it was the first time I clearly encountered the difference between:

- a policy that can move
- and a policy that can choose how to move depending on the environment

The earlier map mostly tested locomotion.
This map exposed the much harder problem of state-dependent behavior selection.

Because of that, this experiment became the conceptual starting point for my later hierarchical RL direction, even though the final explanation turned out to be different from what I first believed.

---

## Later Correction

At the time, I treated this failure as evidence that the search space was too large and that the policy needed a hierarchical structure.

Later, after checking the system more carefully, I found that this interpretation was not actually the main reason for the failure.

The real issue was an observation pipeline problem: the terrain input itself had been connected incorrectly.
Once that input issue was fixed, the problem was resolved much more directly than expected.

So in retrospect, this stage is best understood not as a genuine failure of exploration capacity, but as a case where an observation bug was mistaken for a learning-structure problem.
