## Overview

Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) is an off-policy actor-critic algorithm mainly used in continuous action spaces.
Its defining feature is that it does not maximize reward alone.
Instead, it maximizes **reward + entropy**, which means that the policy is encouraged to find good actions while also avoiding becoming deterministic too early.

This places SAC in the family of **maximum entropy reinforcement learning**.

## 1. Standard RL vs. SAC

A standard reinforcement learning objective can be written as

$$
J(\pi) = \mathbb{E}\left[\sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \gamma^t r(s_t, a_t)\right].
$$

SAC instead optimizes the soft objective

$$
J_{\text{soft}}(\pi)
=
\mathbb{E}\left[
\sum_{t=0}^{\infty}
\gamma^t
\left(
r(s_t, a_t) + \alpha \mathcal{H}(\pi(\cdot \mid s_t))
\right)
\right].
$$

Here,

- $\gamma$ is the discount factor
- $\alpha$ controls the importance of entropy
- $\mathcal{H}(\pi(\cdot \mid s))$ is the policy entropy

So SAC tries to learn a policy that is both:

- high-reward
- not too quickly concentrated on a single action

## 2. What Entropy Means

Policy entropy measures how spread out or stochastic a policy is.

In continuous action spaces,

$$
\mathcal{H}(\pi(\cdot \mid s))
=
- \int \pi(a \mid s)\log \pi(a \mid s)\, da
$$

or equivalently,

$$
\mathcal{H}(\pi(\cdot \mid s))
=
- \mathbb{E}_{a \sim \pi(\cdot \mid s)}[\log \pi(a \mid s)].
$$

This means:

- high entropy $\rightarrow$ broad, stochastic policy
- low entropy $\rightarrow$ sharp, nearly deterministic policy

In practice, SAC does not compute the full integral directly.
Instead, it samples actions from the policy and estimates the entropy term using sampled log-probabilities.

## 3. Main Components of SAC

A standard SAC implementation usually contains:

- one actor
- two critics
- two target critics
- one replay buffer

The actor defines the policy distribution.
The critics estimate soft Q-values.
The target critics provide stable targets.
The replay buffer stores past transitions so that the method can remain off-policy.

A key point is that

$$
\min(Q_1, Q_2)
$$

is not a third critic.
It is simply the more conservative value chosen from the two critics.

## 4. Why Two Critics?

Q-learning methods often suffer from overestimation when function approximation is used.
If Q-values are predicted too optimistically, the actor may be pushed toward poor actions.

SAC reduces this risk by using two critics and taking

$$
\min(Q_{\phi_1}(s,a), Q_{\phi_2}(s,a)).
$$

This makes the update more conservative and helps stabilize learning.

## 5. Why Target Critics?

If critic targets are computed using the current critics directly, the target itself moves too quickly and training becomes unstable.

So SAC uses slowly updated target critics:

$$
\bar{\phi}_i \leftarrow \tau \phi_i + (1-\tau)\bar{\phi}_i,
$$

where $\tau$ is usually very small.

This gives a slowly moving reference target and improves stability.

## 6. SAC Learns a Soft Q, Not a Reward-Only Q

This is one of the most important differences from a standard reward-only interpretation.

SAC does not learn

$$
Q^\pi(s,a) = \mathbb{E}\left[\sum \gamma^t r_t\right].
$$

Instead, it learns a soft value that includes future entropy:

$$
Q^\pi_{\text{soft}}(s,a)
=
\mathbb{E}\left[
\sum \gamma^t
\left(r_t + \alpha \mathcal{H}(\pi(\cdot \mid s_t))\right)
\right].
$$

So the critic values not only future reward, but also whether the future policy remains sufficiently stochastic.

## 7. Why the Entropy Term Appears in the Critic Target

For a transition $(s,a,r,s',d)$, SAC samples a next action

$$
a' \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid s')
$$

and builds the target as

$$
y
=
r
+
\gamma (1-d)
\left(
\min(Q_{\bar{\phi}_1}(s',a'), Q_{\bar{\phi}_2}(s',a'))
-
\alpha \log \pi_\theta(a' \mid s')
\right).
$$

The term

$$
-\alpha \log \pi_\theta(a' \mid s')
$$

is the entropy contribution.

This is not rewarding a rare action by itself.
It is a sample-based approximation of the entropy term in the soft Bellman target.

## 8. Actor Structure in Continuous Control

In practice, the actor usually outputs:

- mean $\mu(s)$
- standard deviation $\sigma(s)$

and samples an action using something like

$$
u = \mu(s) + \sigma(s) \odot \epsilon, \qquad \epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)
$$

followed by

$$
a = \tanh(u)
$$

to keep actions bounded.

So the actor does not output a single action directly.
It outputs a distribution and samples from it.

## 9. Actor Loss

The actor is trained so that sampled actions both receive high critic values and preserve policy entropy.

A common SAC actor loss is

$$
L_\pi
=
\mathbb{E}_{s \sim D,\, a \sim \pi_\theta}
\left[
\alpha \log \pi_\theta(a \mid s)
-
\min(Q_{\phi_1}(s,a), Q_{\phi_2}(s,a))
\right].
$$

This can be read as:

- choose actions with high Q
- do not become too deterministic too early

## 10. Practical Questions and Limits

The most interesting practical questions arise in locomotion and other coordinated continuous-control problems.

### (1) Entropy is useful early, but may be awkward late

In early learning, strong stochasticity helps exploration.
But in late locomotion training, what we often want is:

- consistent foot timing
- stable contacts
- precise joint coordination
- repeatable gait patterns

If entropy remains important too long, the policy may keep injecting stochasticity into a behavior that should become highly stable.

### (2) Critic guides action quality, but not precise variance structure

The critic tells the actor which actions are good.
But it does not directly tell the policy what the ideal state-dependent standard deviation should be.

So variance learning can remain somewhat indirect or coarse.

### (3) Diagonal Gaussian has a structural limitation

In standard SAC, the actor is often modeled as a diagonal Gaussian.
This means each action dimension is perturbed independently.

That is computationally efficient, but in locomotion it can be problematic:
good actions are usually not just individually good joint values, but coordinated whole-body action patterns.

So diagonal Gaussian exploration can become unstructured joint-wise noise rather than structured coordinated exploration.

## 11. Practical Interpretation

This does not mean SAC is weak.
It remains very strong because:

- it is off-policy
- it reuses data efficiently through replay
- exploration is built into the objective
- it is often well suited for high-level decision-making

But for low-level locomotion, SAC can face a structural tension:

- early exploration is a strength
- persistent stochasticity can be a weakness in late precise control

## Final Summary

Soft Actor-Critic is an off-policy maximum entropy actor-critic algorithm.
It learns a stochastic policy and a soft Q-function that includes both reward and future entropy.

Its strengths are clear:

- strong exploration
- high sample efficiency
- powerful continuous-control baseline

Its practical limitations are also important:

- the critic learns soft values, not purely reward-only values
- policy variance is not directly supervised in a precise state-wise way
- diagonal Gaussian exploration may not match strongly coordinated control
- entropy that helps early exploration may conflict with late deterministic locomotion

So SAC is best understood not just as a “better Q-learning method,” but as an algorithm that trades deterministic consolidation for persistent exploration in a principled way.
