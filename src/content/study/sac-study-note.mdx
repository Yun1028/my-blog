## Overview

Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) is an off-policy actor-critic reinforcement learning algorithm mainly used in continuous action spaces.

Its central idea is simple but important:

SAC does not maximize reward alone.  
Instead, it maximizes **reward + entropy**.

This means SAC tries to learn a policy that not only finds good actions, but also avoids becoming too deterministic too early.
Because of this, SAC is best understood as a method from **maximum entropy reinforcement learning** rather than as a standard reward-only actor-critic algorithm.

## 1. Standard RL Objective vs. SAC Objective

In standard reinforcement learning, the objective is usually written as

$$
J(\pi)=\mathbb{E}\left[\sum_{t=0}^{\infty}\gamma^t r(s_t,a_t)\right].
$$

This means we search for a policy that maximizes the discounted sum of rewards.

SAC changes that objective into

$$
J_{\text{soft}}(\pi)
=
\mathbb{E}\left[
\sum_{t=0}^{\infty}
\gamma^t
\left(
r(s_t,a_t)+\alpha \mathcal{H}(\pi(\cdot\mid s_t))
\right)
\right].
$$

Here,

- $\gamma$ is the discount factor
- $\alpha$ controls the importance of entropy
- $\mathcal{H}(\pi(\cdot\mid s))$ is the policy entropy

So SAC prefers a policy that is both:

- high-reward
- not too quickly concentrated on one action only

That is the main philosophical difference between ordinary RL and SAC.

## 2. What Entropy Means

Policy entropy measures how spread out or stochastic a policy is.

In continuous action spaces, entropy can be written as

$$
\mathcal{H}(\pi(\cdot\mid s))
=
-\int \pi(a\mid s)\log \pi(a\mid s)\,da
$$

or equivalently as

$$
\mathcal{H}(\pi(\cdot\mid s))
=
-\mathbb{E}_{a\sim\pi(\cdot\mid s)}[\log \pi(a\mid s)].
$$

This leads to the intuitive interpretation:

- large entropy $\rightarrow$ broad, stochastic policy
- small entropy $\rightarrow$ sharp, nearly deterministic policy

At this point, a natural question appears:

**In a continuous action space, how can we possibly compute this integral over all actions?**

The practical answer is that SAC does **not** compute the full integral directly.
Instead, the actor outputs distribution parameters, usually a mean and a standard deviation,
samples actions from that distribution,
and uses sampled log-probabilities to approximate the expectation term.

So the full entropy is not integrated explicitly.
It is estimated through sampled actions.

## 3. Main Components of SAC

A standard SAC implementation usually contains:

- one actor
- two critics
- two target critics
- one replay buffer

The actor defines the policy distribution.
The critics estimate soft Q-values.
The target critics provide slowly moving targets for stable critic learning.
The replay buffer stores past transitions so that the method can remain off-policy.

A useful summary is:

- actor: 1
- online critics: 2
- target critics: 2

Also, the expression

$$
\min(Q_1,Q_2)
$$

does **not** mean there is a third critic.
It simply means taking the smaller output from the two critics.

## 4. Why SAC Uses Two Critics

Q-learning methods with function approximation often suffer from **overestimation**.
A critic may predict values that are too high,
and then the actor may trust those inflated values and move toward bad actions.

SAC addresses this by maintaining two critics and using

$$
\min(Q_{\phi_1}(s,a),Q_{\phi_2}(s,a)).
$$

This makes the update more conservative and reduces the chance that the actor chases an unrealistically optimistic Q estimate.

## 5. Why Target Critics Are Needed

If critic targets are computed directly from the online critics, the target itself moves too quickly and learning becomes unstable.

So SAC keeps target critics as slowly moving copies:

$$
\bar{\phi}_i \leftarrow \tau \phi_i + (1-\tau)\bar{\phi}_i,
$$

where $\tau$ is usually very small.

The target critic is therefore a slowly updated, more stable reference.
This is one of the mechanisms that makes critic learning less unstable.

## 6. SAC Learns a Soft Q, Not an Ordinary Reward-Only Q

This is one of the most important conceptual points.

SAC does not learn the ordinary Q-function

$$
Q^\pi(s,a)=\mathbb{E}\left[\sum \gamma^t r_t\right].
$$

Instead, it learns a **soft Q-function**:

$$
Q^\pi_{\text{soft}}(s,a)
=
\mathbb{E}\left[
\sum \gamma^t
\left(
r_t+\alpha \mathcal{H}(\pi(\cdot\mid s_t))
\right)
\right].
$$

So the critic is not valuing reward only.
It is valuing reward **plus the future tendency of the policy to remain stochastic**.

That is why SAC can feel conceptually different from ordinary critic-based methods.

## 7. Why the Critic Target Contains an Entropy Term

Suppose we have a transition

$$
(s,a,r,s',d).
$$

At the next state, SAC samples a next action

$$
a' \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot\mid s').
$$

Then the critic target is written as

$$
y
=
r+\gamma(1-d)
\left(
\min(Q_{\bar{\phi}_1}(s',a'),Q_{\bar{\phi}_2}(s',a'))
-\alpha \log \pi_\theta(a'\mid s')
\right).
$$

A very common question is:

**Why is the term $-\alpha \log \pi(a'\mid s')$ inside the target?**

The answer is that SAC is not solving a reward-only Bellman equation.
It is solving a **soft Bellman equation**.
So the critic target must also contain the entropy contribution.

Another natural question is:

**If the actor defines a distribution, why does the target use only one sampled action $a'$?**

The answer is that the true expectation would require integration over the full action distribution,
but this is expensive in practice.
So SAC samples from the actor and uses Monte Carlo approximation for that expected value.

## 8. Does This Mean Rare Actions Are Rewarded?

At first glance, the term

$$
-\alpha \log \pi(a'\mid s')
$$

can look strange.

If an action has low probability, then $\log \pi(a'\mid s')$ is smaller,
so the negative sign can make the entropy contribution larger.
This can create the misleading intuition that SAC is “rewarding rare actions.”

That interpretation is not correct.

The better way to understand it is:

- SAC is not praising one rare action by itself
- it is estimating the entropy of the whole policy through sampled log-probabilities

So a single sample may look noisy,
but over many samples the algorithm is approximating

$$
\mathbb{E}_{a'\sim \pi}[ -\log \pi(a'\mid s') ].
$$

In that sense, the entropy term is not a reward for rare behavior,
but a sample-based measure of policy spread.

## 9. Actor Structure in Continuous Control

In continuous control, the actor usually outputs:

- a mean $\mu(s)$
- a standard deviation $\sigma(s)$

and samples an internal action using

$$
u=\mu(s)+\sigma(s)\odot \epsilon,\qquad \epsilon\sim \mathcal{N}(0,I).
$$

Then the final action is often obtained by

$$
a=\tanh(u)
$$

to keep it bounded.

So the actor does not directly output one deterministic action.
It defines a distribution and samples from it.

## 10. Actor Loss

The actor is trained so that sampled actions both receive high Q-values and preserve sufficient entropy.

A common actor loss is

$$
L_\pi
=
\mathbb{E}_{s\sim D,\; a\sim \pi_\theta}
\left[
\alpha \log \pi_\theta(a\mid s)
-
\min(Q_{\phi_1}(s,a),Q_{\phi_2}(s,a))
\right].
$$

This can be interpreted as:

- choose actions that critics value highly
- but do not become too deterministic too early

So the actor is always pulled between exploitation and stochastic exploration.

## 11. Replay Buffer and Off-Policy Learning

Another important point is that SAC is fully off-policy.

- the critic uses replay-buffer transitions $(s,a,r,s',d)$
- the actor also uses states sampled from the replay buffer
- but the actor samples fresh actions from the current policy

So even the actor is not trained on purely on-policy state distributions.
This is one of the reasons why SAC is much more sample-efficient than PPO.

## 12. Temperature $\alpha$

The coefficient $\alpha$ determines the trade-off between reward and entropy.

- large $\alpha$ $\rightarrow$ entropy is emphasized more
- small $\alpha$ $\rightarrow$ reward is emphasized more

Modern SAC usually does not keep $\alpha$ fixed.
Instead, it often uses **automatic entropy tuning** with a target entropy such as

$$
\mathcal{H}_{\text{target}} \approx -\dim(\mathcal{A}).
$$

Then $\alpha$ is adjusted so that the policy entropy stays near that target.

## 13. Practical Tension in Locomotion

This is where the more practical questions begin.

In locomotion, late-stage control often requires:

- stable contact timing
- precise joint coordination
- repeatable gait
- near-deterministic consistency

But SAC structurally tries to preserve some stochasticity,
because entropy is built directly into its objective.

That means SAC can feel very natural in early exploration,
but somewhat awkward in late-stage deterministic consolidation.

This is not just a vague discomfort.
It is a structural consequence of optimizing a soft objective rather than a purely reward-maximizing one.

## 14. How Variance Is Really Learned

Another practical issue is how the policy standard deviation is learned.

The variance is influenced by two forces:

1. the entropy term, which tries to prevent collapse into a very sharp policy
2. the Q term, which indirectly favors actions sampled from useful regions

So variance is not controlled only by entropy.
It is also affected indirectly through the Q landscape.

But the critic does **not** explicitly teach the actor
what the correct state-dependent standard deviation should be.

So SAC can learn a useful action direction,
while still keeping a variance structure that feels too coarse or too persistent for precise locomotion.

## 15. Structural Limitation of the Diagonal Gaussian Actor

This is one of the most important criticisms in locomotion.

Standard SAC often uses a diagonal Gaussian policy.
That means the policy is factorized across action dimensions.

If the action is 16-dimensional, the policy is effectively treated as if each dimension has its own independent mean and standard deviation.

That is computationally practical, but structurally limited.

In locomotion, a good action is often not just
“good values for 16 independent joints.”
It is a **coordinated whole-body action pattern**.

So diagonal Gaussian entropy can behave more like
independent joint-level shaking
than like structured exploration over meaningful alternative gaits.

## 16. Why Not Model the Full Joint Action Distribution?

A natural question is:

**If coordination matters so much, why not model the full joint action distribution directly?**

In theory, that would be more faithful.
But in practice it becomes difficult because:

- the action space is continuous
- the dimension is high
- sampling must remain efficient
- log-probabilities must be differentiable
- the whole model must remain trainable

So diagonal Gaussian should not be seen as trivially naive.
It is a compromise between tractability and expressive power.

## 17. Practical Summary

So the strengths of SAC are clear:

- off-policy sample efficiency
- strong built-in exploration
- powerful actor-critic structure
- often a natural fit for high-level decision-making

But its limitations also matter, especially in locomotion:

- the critic learns soft values rather than reward-only values
- variance is not explicitly supervised in a precise state-wise way
- diagonal Gaussian exploration may not align with coordinated whole-body control
- entropy that is useful early may become awkward late

This does not mean SAC is weak.
It means SAC is strongest when its objective structure matches the problem structure.
In some tasks, especially high-level control or decision-making, this fit can be very good.
In low-level locomotion, the fit can become less natural in the late stage.

## Final Conclusion

Soft Actor-Critic is best understood not simply as “Q-learning plus stochastic policy,”
but as a principled maximum entropy RL algorithm with both strong strengths and clear structural trade-offs.

Its exploration behavior is one of its biggest advantages,
but that same property can also create tension in settings where the final solution should become highly stable and nearly deterministic.

That is why SAC is both a very powerful baseline and, at the same time,
an algorithm that raises important practical questions in locomotion.
