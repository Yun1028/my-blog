## SAC Summary  
### Basic theory, equations, and the practical questions that naturally arise

## 1. What kind of algorithm is SAC?

Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) is an off-policy actor-critic reinforcement learning algorithm mainly used in continuous action spaces.

Its most important feature is that it does not maximize reward alone.  
Instead, it maximizes

**reward + entropy**.

In other words, SAC follows the philosophy:

> Find good actions, but do not let the policy become deterministic too quickly.

Because of this, SAC is better understood as a method from the family of **maximum entropy reinforcement learning** rather than as a standard reward-only RL algorithm.

## 2. Difference between standard RL and SAC

Standard reinforcement learning usually solves the following objective:

$$
J(\pi)=E\left[\sum_{t=0}^{\infty}\gamma^t r(s_t,a_t)\right]
$$

That is, it searches for a policy that maximizes the discounted sum of rewards.

SAC instead solves

$$
J_{\text{soft}}(\pi)=E\left[\sum_{t=0}^{\infty}\gamma^t \left(r(s_t,a_t)+\alpha H(\pi(\cdot\mid s_t))\right)\right]
$$

where

- $\gamma$: discount factor
- $\alpha$: entropy coefficient
- $H(\pi(\cdot\mid s))$: policy entropy

So SAC learns a policy that is

- high-reward
- but not too quickly concentrated on only one action

## 3. What is entropy?

Policy entropy represents how spread out a policy is, or how stochastic it is.

In continuous action spaces, entropy is theoretically defined as

$$
H(\pi(\cdot\mid s))=-\int \pi(a\mid s)\log \pi(a\mid s)\,da
$$

or equivalently as

$$
H(\pi(\cdot\mid s))=-E_{a\sim \pi(\cdot\mid s)}[\log \pi(a\mid s)]
$$

The meaning is intuitive:

- large entropy → the policy is broadly spread out
- small entropy → the policy is sharp and nearly deterministic

## 4. But how is this integral computed in continuous space?

This was one of the first major questions:

**“In a continuous action space, is it even realistic to compute probabilities over all actions and integrate them?”**

The answer is no: SAC does not compute the full integral directly.

Instead, the actor outputs the parameters of a distribution, usually

- mean $\mu(s)$
- standard deviation $\sigma(s)$

Then actions are sampled from that distribution.

Entropy is approximated through

$$
H(\pi(\cdot\mid s))=-E_{a\sim \pi}[\log \pi(a\mid s)]
$$

using sampled actions and their log-probabilities.

So the key idea is:

- SAC does not directly integrate the full policy distribution
- it approximates the expectation using sampled actions

## 5. Main components of SAC

A standard SAC implementation usually contains:

- actor  
  $\pi_\theta(a\mid s)$  
  a network defining the policy distribution

- two critics  
  $Q_{\phi_1}(s,a), Q_{\phi_2}(s,a)$

- two target critics  
  $Q_{\bar{\phi}_1}(s,a), Q_{\bar{\phi}_2}(s,a)$

- replay buffer  
  stores past transitions

So from a network perspective, SAC typically has

- 2 online critics
- 2 target critics
- 1 actor

An important detail is that

$$
\min(Q_1,Q_2)
$$

does **not** mean a third critic.  
It simply means taking the smaller value between the two critics.

## 6. Why are there two critics?

Q-learning methods often suffer from **Q overestimation** when function approximators are used.

That means the critic may predict a Q-value that is too high compared to reality.

If that happens, the actor may trust that incorrect high Q-value and learn bad actions.

So SAC uses two critics and takes

$$
\min(Q_{\phi_1}(s,a),Q_{\phi_2}(s,a))
$$

to reduce overestimation by using the more conservative estimate.

## 7. Why are target critics needed?

If we build critic targets using the current critics directly, then the target itself changes too quickly, which can make learning unstable.

So SAC keeps target critics separately and updates them slowly:

$$
\bar{\phi}_i \leftarrow \tau \phi_i + (1-\tau)\bar{\phi}_i
$$

where $\tau$ is usually very small, for example

$$
\tau=0.005
$$

So a target critic is a slowly moving stable copy.

## 8. The critic in SAC does not learn an ordinary Q

This is a very important point, and it was also one of the strongest sources of conceptual discomfort.

The critic in SAC does **not** learn a reward-only Q.

That is, it does not learn

$$
Q^\pi(s,a)=E\left[\sum \gamma^t r_t\right]
$$

but instead learns a soft Q corresponding to

$$
Q^\pi_{\text{soft}}(s,a)=E\left[\sum \gamma^t \left(r_t+\alpha H(\pi(\cdot\mid s_t))\right)\right]
$$

So the critic learns not only

- whether reward is large
- but also whether the future policy remains stochastic

## 9. Why does the critic target contain an entropy term?

The critic target is defined as follows.

For a transition

$$
(s,a,r,s',d)
$$

we sample the next action from the actor at the next state:

$$
a' \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot\mid s')
$$

Then the critic target becomes

$$
y=r+\gamma(1-d)\left(\min(Q_{\bar{\phi}_1}(s',a'),Q_{\bar{\phi}_2}(s',a'))-\alpha \log \pi_\theta(a'\mid s')\right)
$$

Here,

$$
-\alpha \log \pi_\theta(a'\mid s')
$$

is the entropy contribution.

This appears because SAC does not solve a reward-only objective, but a reward + entropy objective.

So the critic target itself must already be a soft target.

## 10. First major question here

**“If the actor outputs a distribution rather than a single action, why does the critic target use only one sampled action $a'$?”**

The answer is:

The actor defines a distribution, and the critic target uses one sampled action from that distribution to approximate the expectation.

In theory, we would want to compute

$$
E_{a'\sim \pi(\cdot\mid s')}[Q(s',a')-\alpha \log \pi(a'\mid s')]
$$

But the exact integral is hard.
So in practice SAC does the following:

- the actor defines the distribution
- one action $a'$ is sampled
- that single sample is used to approximate the expectation

So SAC uses a Monte Carlo approximation of the soft Bellman target.

## 11. Second question

**“Then doesn’t a low-probability action become favorable because of $-\log \pi$?”**

At first glance, it can look that way.

Because if an action has low probability, then $\log \pi(a'\mid s')$ becomes smaller, and therefore

$$
-\alpha \log \pi(a'\mid s')
$$

can become larger.

But it would be wrong to interpret this as

> “SAC explicitly rewards rare actions.”

The correct interpretation is:

This is not about praising one individual rare action.  
It is a sample-based approximation of the entropy of the whole policy.

So

$$
-\log \pi(a'\mid s')
$$

should not be interpreted as a reward for a rare action, but as a sampled value reflecting policy spread.

A single sample can be noisy, but over training it approximates

$$
E_{a'\sim \pi}[-\log \pi(a'\mid s')]
$$

## 12. How does the actor produce actions?

In continuous control, the actor usually outputs

- mean $\mu(s)$
- standard deviation $\sigma(s)$

Then it samples

$$
u=\mu(s)+\sigma(s)\odot \epsilon,\qquad \epsilon\sim N(0,I)
$$

and produces a bounded action through

$$
a=\tanh(u)
$$

So the actor does not directly output a single action.
It defines a distribution and samples from it.

## 13. Actor loss

The actor is trained so that its sampled actions

- receive high Q-values
- while still maintaining policy entropy

The actor loss is usually

$$
L_\pi=E_{s\sim D,\;a\sim \pi_\theta}\left[\alpha \log \pi_\theta(a\mid s)-\min(Q_{\phi_1}(s,a),Q_{\phi_2}(s,a))\right]
$$

This can be interpreted as

- $-Q$: choose actions with high Q
- $\alpha \log \pi$: do not become too deterministic too quickly

So the actor is trained to choose actions that critics prefer, while still maintaining policy spread.

## 14. Does only the critic use the replay buffer?

No. The actor also uses it.

- critic: uses full transitions $(s,a,r,s',d)$ from the replay buffer
- actor: uses states $s$ from the replay buffer, and samples fresh actions from the current policy

So the actor is not purely on-policy either.
Its state distribution comes from the replay buffer.

That is why SAC as a whole is off-policy.

## 15. What does temperature $\alpha$ do?

$\alpha$ controls the trade-off between reward and entropy.

- large $\alpha$ → entropy is emphasized more
- small $\alpha$ → reward is emphasized more

Modern SAC usually does not keep $\alpha$ fixed.
Instead, it often uses automatic entropy tuning.

That is, a target entropy is set, often roughly

$$
H_{\text{target}} \approx -\dim(A)
$$

and $\alpha$ is adjusted so that the policy entropy stays near that target.

## 16. Another important discomfort

**“Shouldn’t control eventually become deterministic?”**

This is a very important question.

Yes. In problems like locomotion, where precise control matters, it is natural for the policy to become almost deterministic in the later stage.

But SAC keeps entropy as part of the objective,
so some degree of stochasticity may remain even in the late stage.

That means SAC is very strong for early exploration,
but can feel somewhat uncomfortable in late deterministic consolidation.

This is not just a vague impression.
It is a structural consequence of solving a soft objective.

## 17. Why can this become a problem in late locomotion?

In late locomotion training, what is often needed is

- consistent foot timing
- stable contact
- precise joint coordination
- repeatable gait

But if entropy continues to be maintained, then

- the mean may be good
- but sampled actions may still fluctuate slightly
- and that fluctuation may disrupt the gait

So the entropy term in SAC can be

- an advantage early in training
- but a disadvantage in late precise locomotion

## 18. How is variance actually learned in SAC?

This was another important practical question.

**How is the policy standard deviation $\sigma(s)$ actually learned?**

At first glance, it may seem like entropy alone controls variance,
but in practice there are two forces:

1. the entropy term  
$$
\alpha \log \pi(a\mid s)
$$  
which directly pushes the policy away from becoming too sharp

2. the Q term  
$$
-\min(Q_1,Q_2)
$$  
which indirectly pushes variance depending on how sampled actions are evaluated

So variance is determined by the balance between

- the entropy term trying to preserve spread
- the Q term indirectly pulling the policy toward useful regions

But the critic does **not** directly teach
what the exact correct state-dependent std should be.

That means the critic can indicate good action directions,
but it does not explicitly supervise the ideal variance structure itself.

## 19. Structural limitation of the continuous stochastic actor

One of the most important criticisms we discussed was this:

Standard SAC usually uses a **diagonal Gaussian actor**.

That means if the action is 16-dimensional, each action dimension effectively has its own independent mean and std.

The problem is that in locomotion, a good action is not simply

- “16 individually good joint values”

but rather

- a coordinated whole-body joint action

So diagonal Gaussian entropy can act less like
“structured exploration over meaningful alternative gaits”
and more like
“independent joint-wise noise.”

This is a serious structural weakness for locomotion.

## 20. Why not directly model the full joint distribution?

This leads to another natural question:

**Why not directly model the full joint action distribution?**

In theory, that would be more faithful.

But in practice it is difficult because we need all of the following at once:

- continuous action space
- high dimensionality
- efficient sampling
- tractable log-probability computation
- differentiability
- stable optimization

So the use of diagonal Gaussian should not be seen as simply “dumb.”
It is better understood as a compromise between tractability and expressive power.

## 21. Practical limitations of SAC

If we summarize the concerns above, standard SAC has the following practical limitations:

1. because it learns a soft Q, it can conflict with late deterministic control  
2. it does not directly learn highly precise state-wise variance structure  
3. diagonal Gaussian exploration may not match joint coordination  
4. if entropy remains in the late stage, it may unnecessarily shake an already good gait  

So SAC is extremely strong for early exploration,
but in low-level locomotion there can be a structural discomfort in the late stage.

## 22. Then why is SAC still a strong algorithm?

Even so, SAC remains a powerful method.

The reasons are clear:

1. it is off-policy, so sample efficiency is high  
2. exploration is built directly into the objective  
3. the actor-critic structure works very well in continuous action spaces  
4. it can fit very naturally for navigation, direction choice, or high-level decision-making problems  

So SAC is not a weak algorithm.
It is an algorithm whose objective structure can be a very good match for some problems.

## 23. A structural comparison with PPO

We can roughly compare SAC and PPO like this.

### PPO
- on-policy
- rollout-based
- policy update via advantage
- tends to sharpen toward more deterministic behavior in the late stage
- often practically strong in locomotion

### SAC
- off-policy
- replay-buffer-based
- learns Q directly and guides the actor through it
- maintains entropy structurally
- exploration is strong
- more sensitive to critic quality and entropy design

So, in a very rough summary:

- PPO problem: weak exploration, local optimum
- SAC problem: critic reliability, entropy-induced stochasticity

This perspective makes their structural difference more visible.

## Final Summary

SAC is an off-policy actor-critic algorithm that optimizes a maximum entropy objective including not only reward but also policy entropy.

Because of this, it has major strengths:

- high sample efficiency
- strong exploration
- a powerful baseline in continuous action spaces

But it also has important practical limitations:

- the critic learns a soft Q rather than a reward-only Q
- variance structure is not explicitly supervised in a highly precise way
- diagonal Gaussian policy has structural limits in coordinated control
- in locomotion and other high-dimensional coordinated control problems, exploration can become structurally awkward in the late stage

So SAC is a very powerful algorithm, but especially in low-level locomotion,
one must think carefully about the tension between the strengths of exploration and the need for deterministic consolidation.
